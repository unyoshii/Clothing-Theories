Adornment Theory

The Adornment Theory suggests that clothing is primarily used for decoration and self-expression rather than just practicality. Throughout history, people have adorned themselves with fabrics, colors, and accessories to signify social status, cultural identity, and personal style (Roach-Higgins & Eicher, 1992). From ancient Egyptian jewelry to modern fashion trends, adornment has been a key aspect of human dress.

Fashion serves as a form of nonverbal communication, allowing individuals to express emotions, personality, and group affiliation (Kaiser, 1997). Whether through elaborate designs or minimalist styles, clothing helps people shape their identity and influence how they are perceived.

In conclusion, adornment in clothing goes beyond function, reflecting personal, cultural, and societal values. It remains a powerful tool for self-expression and social distinction.
